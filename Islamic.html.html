Famous Islamic Sites

Cairo is known as the city of a thousand minarets, so imagine how many there are in Egypt as a whole. Spoiler alert: a whole lot.
Egyptian mosques are no cookie cutter experience: some are over 1,000 years old while others were built the day before yesterday, and some are architectural treasures while others are, well, not.
To really experience the beauty of Islamic art, architecture and history, we’ve narrowed down (in no particular order) the Egyptian mosques you should most definitely have on your to-see list.

  1. Mosque of Amr ibn al-As

    2. Amr ibn al-As mosque was the earliest bulding in Egypt, it was constructed in 641 AD by commander Amr Ibn al-As, and it was the first mosque to ever be built in Africa.
    The mosque was built in the location of his tent because a dove nested on his tent and laid an egg, so hi took the dove nest like a good omen of God`s will and this site became the capital of Islamic Egypt.
    The mosque have a modest structure, as it was built using palm leaves, trunks and mud-bricks, it is really hard to know the original appearance of the first building as it was destroyed and rebuilt since the 7th century

    Location: Cairo,Egypt

  2. Mosque of Ahmed Ibn Tulun

    3. This mosque was built by Ahmed Ibn Tulun, an Abbassid governor in Egypt, and it’s said that he was inspired by his homeland of Iraq.It was established in 884 AD.
    It is the oldest mosque in Cairo in its original form and also the largest.It also has one of the very few minarets in the world where the staircase is on the outside and lends to stunning views of the city.

    Location: Cairo,Egypt

  3. Al-Azhar Mosque

    4. Al Azhar was established in 970 AD and it is known as one of the most important, if not *the* most important centers of Islamic theology and learning in the world.
    The mosque and its university for Islamic teaching was founded over 1,000 years ago by the Fatimids who also built the city of Cairo.
    The mosque is famous for its white marble courtyard and five minarets, which were built in 1340, 1469, and 1510.

    Location: Cairo,Egypt

  4. Al-Sahaba Mosque

    5. The newest mosque on this list as it was established in 2017,amix of Ottoman, Fatimid and Mamluk architectural and interior design styles, some find the mosque to be extremely beautiful while others find it garish.
    It is located between the Red Sea and Sharm’s mountains, and can hold over 3,000 visitors.

    Location: Sharm El Sheikh,Egypt

  5. Mosque of Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi

    6. This mosque was built over the tomb of the Andalusian Islamic Scholar and Sheikh Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi who left his home country of spain in 1242 for tunisia before moving to Alexandria where he spent 43 years until his death.
    This mosque was built in 1775 by Algerian Sheikh Abu Hassan el-Maghreby and it remains to date Alexandria`s largest mosque.

    Location: Alexandria,Egypt

  6. Mosque of Muhammad Ali

    7. Located in the Citadel, this mosque was built between 1824 and 1857 in the Ottoman style by Mohammad Ali Pasha, a ruler of Egypt.
    It was designed by The architect Yousif Boushnaq, a Turkish man who came especially from Istanbul and built this great mosque for Mohamed Ali, he then was buried in the tomb situated on the southern-east side of Beit Al Salah, to the right hand side of the entrance which leads to the main section.
    This mosque is also called the Alabaster Mosque because of the shining marble which covers its inner and outer walls. it is rectangular in shape and it consists of two sections: The Eastern Section, the main section ( Beit al Salah ) and the Western Section or the courtyard (the Sahn). The Eastern section is the part which is dedicated for the prayer, also it includes the Mohamed Ali's Tomb, while the Western section is a large open courtyard of about 177 feet in length and 173 feet in width.

    Location:Old Cairo,Egypt

  7. Al-Rifai Mosque

    8. Al-Rifai mosque was built in 1869-1912,right next to the Sultan Hassan mosque, separated by only a small pedestrian lane.It also hosts the tomb of its namesake, the Sheikh Al Rifai, a medieval Islamic saint.
    There’s also a mausoleum for the Shah of Iran.

    Location: Cairo,Egypt

  8. El-Mina Mosque

    9. El Mina translates to ‘the port’, and it’s a fitting name for this mosque built right on the Hurghada Red Sea harbor in 2012.
    Built over an area of 4,000 meters, this mosque has 25 domes, a large marble courtyard and two towering minarets. The architectural style is eclectic, with nods to different Islamic motifs and designs.

    Location: Hurghada,Egypt
